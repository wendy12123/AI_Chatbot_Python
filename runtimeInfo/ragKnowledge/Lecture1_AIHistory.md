# AI Fundamentals: Introduction, History, and Modern Landscape

## 1. Defining Artificial Intelligence

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is a broad field with definitions that have evolved since its inception.

* **John McCarthy's Definition (1955)**: The goal of AI is to develop machines that behave as though they were intelligent.
* **Encyclopedia Britannica (1991)**: AI is the ability of digital computers or computer-controlled robots to solve problems normally associated with higher intellectual processing capabilities of humans.
* **The "Moving Target" Definition**: AI is often defined as "whatever has not been done yet" (e.g., once optical character recognition became common, it was often no longer considered "true" AI).
* **Core Objective**: The ability of a system to calculate, reason, perceive relationships and analogies, learn from experience, and store/retrieve information from memory.

---

## 2. Artificial Intelligence vs. Machine Learning vs. Deep Learning

It is helpful to view the relationship between these terms as a set of nested circles:

* **Artificial Intelligence (AI)**: The overarching field. Programs with the ability to learn and reason like humans.
* **Machine Learning (ML)**: A subset of AI. Algorithms whose performance improves as they are exposed to more data over time.
  * *Traditional ML*: Focuses on learning patterns from labeled data using algorithms like decision trees or linear regression.
* **Deep Learning (DL)**: A subset of ML. Multilayered neural networks that learn from vast amounts of data. It leverages hierarchical representations to master complex patterns.

---

## 3. Categories of AI

AI can be categorized based on its breadth of capability:

* **Artificial Narrow Intelligence (ANI)**: Also known as "Weak AI." AI that is specialized in one functional area (e.g., Siri, Alexa, or a chess-playing program). This represents all AI currently in existence.
* **Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)**: Also known as "Strong AI." A hypothetical AI that possesses the ability to perform any intellectual task a human can do.
* **Artificial Super Intelligence (ASI)**: A hypothetical state where AI surpasses human intelligence across all fields, including creativity and social skills.

---

## 4. The Evolution of AI Paradigms

The field is currently undergoing a shift from "Traditional AI" to "Advanced AI":

* **Traditional AI/Machine Learning**: Focuses on simple prediction and pattern recognition based on historical data.
* **Advanced AI (Generative AI)**: Represents a paradigm shift toward complex understanding. These systems can:
  * Reason about context.
  * Process sequential information.
  * Generate entirely novel content (text, images, code).

---

## 5. Timeline and "AI Winters"

AI history is characterized by cycles of hype and disappointment:

* **Late 1950s - 1960s**: Initial optimism and founding of the field.
* **AI Winters**: Periods where funding and interest dried up because AI failed to meet its lofty expectations.
* **Modern Era**: Driven by the "Three Pillars of AI Success":
    1. **Algorithms**: Improved mathematical models and neural network architectures.
    2. **Data**: The explosion of "Big Data" provides the fuel for training.
    3. **Computing Power**: Advances in hardware (GPUs and TPUs) allow for the processing of massive datasets.
